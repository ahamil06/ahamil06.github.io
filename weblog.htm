The Ideal Home 1 - July 11th, 2023

  

   It has come to my attention within recent times that a need to document certain information in writing exists; future reference will benefit myself and one who cares to read greatly. Certain information which I wish to keep in mind years from now is more prone to being lost to time than others as is fairly obvious. That being said, there exist certain elements of a home which are most appealing for their practical usage. In my writing I will record these features of the ideal home according to my thoughts.

Bathroom Tiling

   Subway tiles spanning the floor to the walls up to the top of bathroom ceilings should be made a priority to install. Both ease of cleanliness and visual appeal stem from these tiles. Ideally, white ceramic tiles of the same kind should be present. Recent experiences I have had with various bathroom configurations too brings forth that which one should avoid in their quarters. Any wallpaper or painted walls have, to my knowledge, been much more prone to the trapping of bacteria and moisture in various pores. The smooth, sealed surface of ceramic tiles is easily cleaned while being functionally waterproof. Steam production from showering and the generally increased probability of water coming into contact with the wall in the bathroom makes these properties most desirable. To my horror, I have recently observed a bathroom configuration which defied my established principles to the greatest extent. This was located in an older Maine home, presumably built in the early 20th century. Although it was but a brief look, I immediately saw the worst of bathroom design. Wallpaper covered the walls of this room, and a fabric cushion was located to the right of the shower, separated by a wall. A window sat behind this seating area. This raised cushion area was elevated by a wooden cabinet. Although I cannot recall what flooring was present with certainty, I believe a wooden floor adulterated the ground. I must make another important note that wood of any kind should be avoided as much as reasonable in the bathroom. Like wallpaper or fabric, wood has no shortage of pores in which moisture and bacteria is caught. If sealed correctly, wood provides no difficulty. It was evident that the wood found in the aforementioned bathroom was not sealed to an extent where moisture is sufficiently halted. At one time it may have, though the years have made necessary another coating or sealant. One who instals ceramic subway tiles in his home restrooms, spaces as close as the installation procedure permits without copious difficulty, will find my advice to be true.

It must be noted that my writing occurred during the vacation I had with my family in Maine. Some of the information, namely the bathroom with the most abhorrent design, has been recorded during this vacation due to being so recent in memory.
